Tablut was one of a family of "tafl" games played by the Vikings, Anglo-Saxons and other ancient peoples of northern Europe. Although the rules are simple, the game involves considerable skill.

Tafl games use various different boards and configurations of pieces, but all have in common a 2:1 ratio between attackers and defenders. The goal for the defending side is to rescue the King by moving him to the edge of the board, while the attackers attempt to surround him.

The precise rules played in ancient times are unknown. In its simplest form the game is asymmetrical, with an advantage for White. Additional rules, such as an "unarmed" King, are necessary to even up the stakes. In many variants of the game, the King must reach the corner of the board, not just the edge.

Tablut largely disappeared after the arrival of chess in northern Europe in the 12th century, but the Swedish naturalist Linnaeus saw it played on a reindeer skin board in Lapland as late as 1732. In a nod to current events, the Lapps called the two sides 'Swedes' and 'Muscovites' - the Muscovites being the attackers.

  • All pieces, including the White King, move like the rook in chess (any number of squares vertically or horizontally in any direction; there are no diagonal moves). No piece can jump over another.
  • White wins by moving the King to the edge of the board. Black wins by surrounding the King on all four sides.
  • Any piece other than the King can be captured by trapping it between two enemy pieces - either from above and below or from the left and the right. The captured piece is then removed from the board.
  • It is also possible to capture a piece by trapping it against one of the 17 shaded squares. Black can win by surrounding the King on only two or three sides if the others are blocked by shaded squares.
  • The King can neither capture nor be captured in the ordinary way.
  • Once the shaded squares have been vacated by the pieces that start there, they can never be reoccupied. Black pieces can, however, move around within their own base camps. Any piece can pass through the shaded squares without stopping there.
